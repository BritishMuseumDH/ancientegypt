The Scribe's Tools

 

   Writing Explore

 

Explain what a scribe is and introduce the site. Then, give each child a copy of the site trail.

 

The only people who could read and write in ancient Egypt were called scribes. People usually became scribes only if their father had been a scribe. They attended a scribal school where they learnt how to read and write.

When scribes left scribe school there were many opportunities open to them. As it was necessary to understand texts and correspondence, all priests, government officials and record-keepers were scribes.

Most ancient Egyptian scribes were men. There is some evidence of female doctors, who would have had to be able to read medical texts; however, the number of female scribes was probably few.

Scribes had many different pieces of equipment: a palette which held their reed pens and two cakes of ink, a papyrus scroll for recording information, a briefcase which held their palette, extra papyrus scrolls and ink, and a document box which held old records.

Papyrus was one of the most common materials used for writing, but it was expensive. It was lightweight and flexible, which was important when it was used for record-keeping. Papyrus was also easy to store because the scrolls were not bulky. Although papyrus was a common writing material, it was not wasted; scribes often used both sides of a papyrus scroll for keeping records so that they did not waste the papyrus.
